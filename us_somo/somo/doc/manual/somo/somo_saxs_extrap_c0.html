Extrapolation to Zero Concentration:

Last updated: July 2026

Synopsis

Small-angle scattering intensities measured at finite concentration contain a concentration-dependent contribution from interparticle interactions (the structure factor). The Extrapolate to zero concentration function removes this contribution by combining a set of I(q) curves measured at different concentrations and extrapolating, at each q, to the ideal dilute limit c = 0.

The function is reached from the SOMO SAXS/SANS Load, manage, and process SAS data module: load or plot the curves of interest, press Load Plotted, select at least three curves, and choose Extrap. to zero conc → Extrapolate. A dialog then opens in which concentrations are assigned and the extrapolation is performed.

The concentration and curve-selection dialog

The dialog lists each selected curve with its concentration. Concentrations are pre-filled from the curve headers (the Conc: tag) or from any value already known to SOMO, and may be edited; each must be a non-negative number. A concentration shown on a red background is not yet a valid number.

Selecting the curves. The rows highlighted (selected) in the table are the curves that will be used. All rows are selected by default. Use the mouse (click, Shift-click for a range, Ctrl-click to toggle individual rows) to change the selection. The Extrapolate button becomes active only when at least three rows are selected and every selected row has a valid concentration; the status line below the table reports the current state.

Note: because the concentration cells remain editable, double-clicking a cell to edit it may collapse the selection to that single row. Set the concentrations first, then choose the selection.

Extrapolation modes

Two modes are offered, selected with the Absolute-scale mode checkbox:

Zimm mode (checkbox unchecked, the default). At each q the concentration-normalized intensity I(q,c)/c is fitted linearly against c and the c = 0 intercept is reported. This is the small-angle-scattering analogue of a Zimm plot. The output is a concentration-normalized curve (intensity per unit concentration); it is tagged Conc:1, SOMO's convention for an already-normalized curve, and carries the standard error of the fitted intercept as its uncertainty.

Automatic slope regularization (GCV) (checkbox, on by default; applies to both modes). The interparticle/interaction term — the fit's concentration slope — varies smoothly with q and dies off at high q, so it can be regularized across q without harming the form-factor detail carried by the intercept. With this option on, all q are fitted jointly with a smoothness penalty applied to only the slope α(q) (the intercept Iex(q) is left untouched), and the smoothing strength is chosen automatically by Generalized Cross-Validation (GCV) — there is nothing to tune. On simulated dilution series with a known dilute-limit ground truth this tracks the best-possible smoothing across weak/strong and repulsive/attractive interactions, and on real data it yields a markedly cleaner low-q Guinier region than independent per-q fits. The editor window reports the selected penalty and the effective degrees of freedom. Uncheck the box for the classic independent per-q fits (optionally with the manual window below).

Concentration model (drop-down, Zimm mode only). This selects how the intensity is fitted against concentration at each q, and chiefly affects the accuracy of the molecular weight read from the extrapolated forward scatter I(0):

  • Additive I(q)/c (the default). Fits I(q,c)/c linearly against c and reports the c = 0 intercept — the classic Zimm-plot form. This gives the cleanest low-q profile, but because the second-virial structure factor makes I/c a saturating (convex) function of c, a straight-line fit reads I(0) — and hence the molecular weight — systematically low when interparticle interactions are strong.
  • Reciprocal c/I(q). Fits c/I(q,c) linearly against c and reports I(0) = 1/(intercept). Because the second-virial relation c/I = (1 + 2A2Mc)/I0 is linear in c, this removes the additive fit's low bias and gives an unbiased forward scatter, i.e. a more accurate molecular weight. The uncertainty is propagated as SE(1/u) = SE(u)/u2.
  • 2nd-order virial c/I(q). Adds a c2 term (c/I = u + vc + wc2) to absorb the residual curvature that appears at strong interaction, where the first-order reciprocal begins to over-correct. It is the most robust choice across weak-to-strong interaction but needs at least four to five well-spread concentrations to determine the extra parameter; with fewer, prefer the first-order reciprocal.

All three were validated against a physical Percus–Yevick hard-sphere ground truth: the additive fit is biased low, the first-order reciprocal is accurate at realistic interaction but over-corrects when very strong, and the second-order virial is within a few percent across the whole range. The additive model is unchanged from earlier versions and remains the default, so existing workflows are unaffected. Absolute-scale mode always uses the additive fit. A preferred default may be preselected via the gparam saxs_extrap_c0_model (0 additive, 1 reciprocal, 2 virial).

Fit broadening (manual, Zimm mode). An alternative to the automatic GCV option, used only when GCV is off (and Absolute-scale is off). Setting Zimm fit broadening (q-window) to a value N > 1 averages the concentration slope over an N-point window and then recomputes each intercept (a = ⟨I/c⟩ − bsmoothed⟨c⟩). 0 (the default of this field) leaves the per-q fits independent. As with GCV, this smooths the concentration dependence, not the output curve itself (smoothing the output directly would smear real features and is not done). The field is disabled while either automatic GCV or Absolute-scale mode is selected.

Absolute-scale mode (checkbox checked). This follows the ATSAS almerge approach (Petoukhov et al., 2012; Franke et al., 2017). The highest-concentration curve is taken as the reference and every curve is scaled to it by cref/ci. At each q the scaled intensity is fitted linearly against concentration and the c = 0 intercept — the infinite-dilution intensity — is taken. Crucially, this extrapolation is applied only in the low-q region where the curves show a real concentration dependence; above the merging point the reference curve is used verbatim. The merging point is found automatically as the first q from which the extrapolated curve agrees with the reference to within the reference's own errors — a reduced χ2 of (Iex − Iref)/σref below a threshold (default 4, i.e. ~2σ agreement; set via saxs_extrap_c0_merge_chi2). This error-band test is used in preference to a sign-run (CORMAP) test because the latter, comparing the smoothed extrapolation against the noisy reference, is biased toward placing the merge too high; CORMAP (saxs_extrap_c0_pvalue_alpha, default 0.01) is used as a fallback when the reference curve carries no error column. This removes the interparticle/structure-factor effect at low q while keeping stable, positive intensities at high q (a per-point fit there would only track noise and can go negative). The result is on the reference curve's absolute intensity scale and is tagged with the reference concentration. Its error bars are the reference curve's uncertainty at and above the merging point (where the output is the reference) and the propagated standard error of the fitted intercept below it (the extrapolated region), so the low-q extrapolation carries its own uncertainty rather than borrowing the reference's.

Per-q regression plots

Checking Show per-q regression plots in the concentration dialog opens a pop-up window after the extrapolation. It shows, one q value at a time, each input curve as a point at its concentration (with its error bar) together with the per-q result: the fitted line and its extrapolated c = 0 intercept (highlighted, with its own error bar) at the dashed vertical axis. In Absolute-scale mode the title also notes, for the current q, whether the output there is the extrapolated intercept (below the merging point) or taken from the reference curve (at/above it). Drag the wheel to scroll smoothly through the q grid (it has inertia, so a flick keeps scrolling); the Prev and Next buttons step one q at a time. The line below the plot reports, for the current q, the extrapolated c = 0 result ± its error, the number of curves used, and a goodness-of-fit summary — the coefficient of determination R², and (when the data carry error bars) the reduced χ² of the points about the fit — so the quality of each per-q extrapolation can be judged at a glance.

The y axis and the fitted curve follow the selected model and mode. For the additive model the axis is I(q)/c (or I*(q) for already-normalized inputs, or I(q) scaled to the reference concentration in Absolute-scale mode) and the intercept is the dilute I(0) directly. For the reciprocal and 2nd-order virial models the axis is c/I(q): the plotted intercept is u and the readout also gives the recovered forward scatter I(0) = 1/u with its propagated error; the 2nd-order virial draws its fitted parabola rather than a straight line. When automatic GCV regularization (or the manual fit-broadening window) is active, the fit line uses the globally-smoothed slope — a note above the plot says so, and reports the effective slope degrees of freedom for GCV — so the line reflects the regularized fit and may not pass exactly through the raw per-q intercept. This is a diagnostic view; it does not add or change any plotted curve.

Output

A new curve is added to the plot. Its name is the longest common prefix of the selected curve names followed by _extrap_c0 and a method token: _zimm or _absolute (for example Sub_..._extrap_c0_absolute). If a curve of that name already exists a numeric suffix is appended. Curves with a concentration of zero are excluded from the fit, and any q point with fewer than two usable values is skipped; the editor window reports how many curves and points were used, and, in Absolute-scale mode, the reference curve and the per-curve scale factors.

Guinier quality check. After the curve is produced, the editor window reports a Guinier fit of it (a straight-line fit of ln I versus q2 over the low-q region, the window chosen so qmax·Rg ≤ 1.3): the radius of gyration Rg, I(0), and the fit's R2. This is a ground-truth-free quality signal — a high R2 with a physically sensible Rg indicates a clean low-q extrapolation; a low R2 is flagged and suggests checking the low-q data or the assigned concentrations.

I*(q) (already-normalized) curves

Curves whose name contains _Istarq_ are treated as already being in I*(q) form (header units g/mol, I(0) = MW), i.e. their intensity has already been normalized by concentration — even though their Conc: header carries the real, non-unity measurement concentration. For these curves the intensity is used as is (it is not divided by concentration again), while the real concentration is still used as the extrapolation variable. A selection made entirely of I*(q) curves therefore produces an already-normalized result (tagged Conc:1 in both modes). Mixing I*(q) curves with raw-intensity curves in one extrapolation is flagged with a warning, as the two are on different scales.

References

The linear extrapolation of concentration-normalized intensity to infinite dilution (Zimm mode) follows the approach introduced in: Zimm, B. H. (1948) The scattering of light and the radial distribution function of high polymer solutions. J. Chem. Phys. 16, 1093-1099.

The concentration extrapolation and the almerge algorithm reproduced by Absolute-scale mode are described in: Petoukhov, M. V., Franke, D., Shkumatov, A. V., Tria, G., Kikhney, A.
