Extrapolation to Zero Concentration:

Last updated: July 2026

Synopsis

Small-angle scattering intensities measured at finite concentration contain a concentration-dependent contribution from interparticle interactions (the structure factor). The Extrapolate to zero concentration function removes this contribution by combining a set of I(q) curves measured at different concentrations and extrapolating, at each q, to the ideal dilute limit c = 0.

The function is reached from the SOMO SAXS/SANS Load, manage, and process SAS data module: load or plot the curves of interest, press Load Plotted, select at least three curves, and choose Extrap. to zero conc → Extrapolate. A dialog then opens in which concentrations are assigned and the extrapolation is performed.

The concentration and curve-selection dialog

The dialog lists each selected curve with its concentration. Concentrations are pre-filled from the curve headers (the Conc: tag) or from any value already known to SOMO, and may be edited; each must be a non-negative number. A concentration shown on a red background is not yet a valid number.

Selecting the curves. The rows highlighted (selected) in the table are the curves that will be used. All rows are selected by default. Use the mouse (click, Shift-click for a range, Ctrl-click to toggle individual rows) to change the selection. The Extrapolate button becomes active only when at least three rows are selected and every selected row has a valid concentration; the status line below the table reports the current state.

Note: because the concentration cells remain editable, double-clicking a cell to edit it may collapse the selection to that single row. Set the concentrations first, then choose the selection.

Extrapolation modes

An extrapolation is described by two independent choices: how the intensity is fitted against concentration (the concentration model, below) and what convention the resulting curve is expressed in (two checkboxes, described here). The fit itself always produces the same thing — the c = 0 dilute-limit curve, normalized per unit concentration (the small-angle-scattering analogue of a Zimm plot). With both output checkboxes unchecked (the default), that normalized curve is the output: it is tagged Conc:1 (SOMO's convention for an already-normalized curve) and carries the standard error of the fitted intercept as its uncertainty.

Automatic slope regularization (GCV) (checkbox, on by default; applies to both modes). The interparticle/interaction term — the fit's concentration slope — varies smoothly with q and dies off at high q, so it can be regularized across q without harming the form-factor detail carried by the intercept. With this option on, all q are fitted jointly with a smoothness penalty applied to only the slope α(q) (the intercept Iex(q) is left untouched), and the smoothing strength is chosen automatically by Generalized Cross-Validation (GCV) — there is nothing to tune. On simulated dilution series with a known dilute-limit ground truth this tracks the best-possible smoothing across weak/strong and repulsive/attractive interactions, and on real data it yields a markedly cleaner low-q Guinier region than independent per-q fits. The editor window reports the selected penalty and the effective degrees of freedom. Uncheck the box for the classic independent per-q fits (optionally with the manual window below).

Weight regression by curve errors (1/σ²) (checkbox, on by default). With this on, every concentration regression is fitted with inverse-variance weights — each point weighted by 1/σ², with the curve's error propagated onto the fit axis (σ/c for the additive I/c axis, σ·c/I² for the reciprocal c/I axis) — and that error is propagated into the extrapolated intercept's reported error bar. This is the statistically optimal choice when the error bars are reliable, and for the reciprocal axis it automatically de-emphasizes the noisy high-q. Unchecked, an ordinary (unweighted) least-squares fit is used: the curve errors are still drawn in the regression plots but are not used in the fit, and the intercept error bar is the fit's residual scatter. Unweighted is more robust when the error bars are missing or mis-scaled (common in SAXS); a large difference between the weighted and unweighted results is itself a sign that the error bars are driving the extrapolation. The switch affects only the concentration regression (including the outlier check, which then standardizes residuals by their per-q scatter instead of by σ) — not the high-q reference merge test or the post-fit SD reassessment, which are inherently error-based. If weighting is on but some points carry no usable error, those q are fitted unweighted and the count is reported. An unweighted run adds _uw to the output curve name.

Concentration model (drop-down). This selects how the intensity is fitted against concentration at each q, and chiefly affects the accuracy of the molecular weight read from the extrapolated forward scatter I(0):

  • Additive I(q)/c. Fits I(q,c)/c linearly against c and reports the c = 0 intercept — the classic Zimm-plot form. This gives the cleanest low-q profile, but because the second-virial structure factor makes I/c a saturating (convex) function of c, a straight-line fit reads I(0) — and hence the molecular weight — systematically low when interparticle interactions are strong.
  • Reciprocal c/I(q) (the default). Fits c/I(q,c) linearly against c and reports I(0) = 1/(intercept). Because the second-virial relation c/I = (1 + 2A2Mc)/I0 is linear in c, this removes the additive fit's low bias and gives an unbiased forward scatter, i.e. a more accurate molecular weight. The uncertainty is propagated as SE(1/u) = SE(u)/u2.
  • 2nd-order virial c/I(q). Adds a c2 term (c/I = u + vc + wc2) to absorb the residual curvature that appears at strong interaction, where the first-order reciprocal begins to over-correct. It is the most robust choice across weak-to-strong interaction but needs at least four to five well-spread concentrations to determine the extra parameter; with fewer, prefer the first-order reciprocal.

All three were validated against a physical Percus–Yevick hard-sphere ground truth: the additive fit is biased low, the first-order reciprocal is accurate at realistic interaction but over-corrects when very strong, and the second-order virial is within a few percent across the whole range. The reciprocal model is the default, since an unbiased molecular weight is the more common need; the additive model remains available for work that values the smoothest low-q profile over MW accuracy. The model applies regardless of the output convention, so the reference-scale and spliced outputs use the selected model too. A preferred default may be preselected via the gparam saxs_extrap_c0_model (0 additive, 1 reciprocal, 2 virial).

Fit broadening (manual, Zimm mode). An alternative to the automatic GCV option, used only when GCV is off (and both output-convention boxes are off). Setting Zimm fit broadening (q-window) to a value N > 1 averages the concentration slope over an N-point window and then recomputes each intercept (a = ⟨I/c⟩ − bsmoothed⟨c⟩). 0 (the default of this field) leaves the per-q fits independent. As with GCV, this smooths the concentration dependence, not the output curve itself (smoothing the output directly would smear real features and is not done). The field is disabled while automatic GCV or either output-convention box is selected.

Output conventions (two independent checkboxes). The normalized c = 0 fit above can be re-expressed in either or both of the following ways. They are output conventions layered on top of the fit — they do not change the fit, so they combine freely with any concentration model.

  • Output on reference (absolute) scale. Multiply the extrapolated curve by the highest concentration cref so it is expressed on that reference curve's absolute intensity scale, tagged with the reference concentration (dividing by it recovers the normalized form). Because the normalized fit times cref is exactly the infinite-dilution intensity on the reference scale, this is a pure re-scaling.
  • Splice reference curve at high q (the ATSAS almerge idea; Petoukhov et al., 2012; Franke et al., 2017). Apply the extrapolation only in the low-q region where the curves show a real concentration dependence, and above a merging point take the highest-concentration reference curve verbatim — replacing the noisy high-q extrapolated tail (a per-point fit there only tracks noise and can go negative) with the reference's stable, positive data, and carrying its error bars there. The merging point is found automatically as the first q from which the extrapolation agrees with the reference to within the reference's own errors — a reduced χ2 of (Iex − Iref)/σref below a threshold (default 4, ~2σ agreement; saxs_extrap_c0_merge_chi2). This error-band test is preferred to a sign-run (CORMAP) test, which — comparing the smoothed extrapolation against the noisy reference — is biased toward placing the merge too high; CORMAP (saxs_extrap_c0_pvalue_alpha, default 0.01) is the fallback when the reference has no error column. Below the merge the error bars are the propagated intercept standard error; at and above it (where the output is the reference) they are the reference's own.

The two combine to give four behaviors: neither box = the classic normalized Conc:1 curve; scale only = the same curve on the absolute reference scale; splice only = a normalized curve with the clean reference tail; both = the former "absolute-scale mode" (reference scale with the high-q splice). Unlike before, all four now honor the chosen concentration model, so the reciprocal/virial models' unbiased molecular weight is available on the absolute scale too.

Recompute output SDs (drop-down). By default the extrapolated curve carries its propagated error bars (the per-q intercept standard error, and the reference's own errors in any spliced high-q region). This optional selection instead reassesses the output curve's error bars from the curve's own point-to-point scatter, using the same BayesApp-style engine as the HPLC/KIN s.d.util “SD rescale” button (Larsen & Pedersen, 2021): a local inverse-variance quadratic smoother gives the residuals and an effective number of degrees of freedom, hence a reduced χ2 and a two-sided p-value, and σ is rescaled only when the fit is poor and the implied factor is more than 10 % from unity. The modes are Constant (a single factor for all q), Non-constant (a per-q-bin factor, for a q-dependent misestimate), and Intensity-dependent (σ → σ + a·|I|); Off keeps the propagated errors. When a reference tail is spliced in, the reassessment spans the whole spliced curve. The verdict, reduced χ2, and p-value are reported in the log, and the chosen mode is encoded in the curve name (_sdC / _sdN / _sdI).

Automatically discard one outlier concentration (checkbox, off by default; with two threshold fields). A single curve with a mis-entered concentration, a scaling error, or aggregation sits off the concentration trend at essentially every q, and because it is on the wrong concentration axis rather than merely noisy, the inverse-variance weighting does not suppress it — it biases every per-q fit and can even be picked as the high-q reference. When this box is checked, such a curve is detected and removed from the whole fit (before the reference is chosen), so the extrapolation is computed on the remaining curves. At most one curve is ever discarded, and only when enough remain (≥ 3, or ≥ 4 for the 2nd-order virial). Detection is at the curve level, never per-q: per-q outlier trimming would overfit at these small point counts and stitch discontinuities into the output, whereas a bad concentration is a whole-curve property. The two fields tune it: Outlier threshold (sigma) (default 3.0) is the mean standardized residual across q above which a curve is flagged, and Min χ² gain to discard (default 1.5) is the factor by which removing the curve must improve the pooled reduced χ2 before it is actually dropped; the curve must also be consistently one-signed (a systematic offset, not noise). Because a concentration error bites hardest at low q, mild outliers often sit near a mean residual of 1–2 rather than 3, so lowering the sigma field (e.g. to 1.5) with the χ² gain near 1.2 is the way to act on a borderline curve; the default is deliberately conservative. Every run reports the most-outlying curve and all of these numbers in the log (so the thresholds can be tuned), whether or not anything is discarded. A discarded curve is drawn as a red x in the per-q regression plots and noted in the log, and the output name gains _qc1. This targets concentration/scale outliers; curve-shape problems such as aggregation are the job of the Guinier quality check below.

Per-q regression plots

Checking Show per-q regression plots in the concentration dialog opens a pop-up window after the extrapolation. It shows, one q value at a time, each input curve as a point at its concentration (with its error bar) together with the per-q result: the fitted line and its extrapolated c = 0 intercept (highlighted, with its own error bar) at the dashed vertical axis. When the reference curve is spliced (the merge checkbox), the title also notes, for the current q, whether the output there is the extrapolated intercept (below the merging point) or taken from the reference curve (at/above it). Drag the wheel to scroll smoothly through the q grid (it has inertia, so a flick keeps scrolling); the Prev and Next buttons step one q at a time. The line below the plot reports, for the current q, the extrapolated c = 0 result ± its error, the number of curves used, and a goodness-of-fit summary — the coefficient of determination R², and (when the data carry error bars) the reduced χ² of the points about the fit — so the quality of each per-q extrapolation can be judged at a glance.

The y axis and the fitted curve follow the selected concentration model; the plot always shows the normalized fit (the reference scale and splice are output conventions applied afterwards, not part of the fit). For the additive model the axis is I(q)/c (or I*(q) for already-normalized inputs) and the intercept is the dilute I(0) directly. For the reciprocal and 2nd-order virial models the axis is c/I(q): the plotted intercept is u and the readout also gives the recovered forward scatter I(0) = 1/u with its propagated error; the 2nd-order virial draws its fitted parabola rather than a straight line. When automatic GCV regularization (or the manual fit-broadening window) is active, the fit line uses the globally-smoothed slope — a note above the plot says so, and reports the effective slope degrees of freedom for GCV — so the line reflects the regularized fit and may not pass exactly through the raw per-q intercept. This is a diagnostic view; it does not add or change any plotted curve.

A negative R² is expected at some q and is not an error. R² here is the fraction of the points' variance explained by the plotted line (1 − SSresidual/SStotal), so it goes negative whenever the plotted line fits the points more poorly than a flat line through their mean. That happens legitimately in two situations. First, with GCV or fit broadening the slope at each q is deliberately borrowed from neighbouring q rather than fitted locally; at the noise-dominated low-q points — where the individual curves show no real concentration trend — that shared slope can fit worse than a flat mean, giving R² < 0. This is the regularization working as intended: it stabilizes the c = 0 intercept by not chasing local noise, and a negative R² there is a useful signal that the per-q trend is noise rather than a defect. Second, the fit is inverse-variance weighted while R² is computed unweighted, so a single high-error point can also push it slightly below zero. With only a few concentrations the statistic is noisy in any case; judge the extrapolation on the reduced χ² and the overall Guinier quality check as well, not on a single q's R².

Output

A new curve is added to the plot. Its name is the longest common prefix of the selected curve names followed by _extrap_c0 and tokens encoding the choices that produced it — the concentration model (_add / _recip / _virial2), then _abs if on the reference scale and _merge if the reference was spliced — and, when spliced, the merge point q (with the decimal written as an underscore, e.g. _merge_q0_0898 for a cutover at q = 0.0898), and the SD-reassessment mode if one was applied (_sdC / _sdN / _sdI), and _qc1 if an outlier concentration was discarded, and _uw if the regression was unweighted (curve errors not used), so a run's cutover is visible in its name (for example Sub_..._extrap_c0_recip_abs_merge_q0_0898). Distinct selections therefore produce distinct, self-describing names; a genuine repeat of the same configuration gets a numeric suffix. Curves with a concentration of zero are excluded from the fit, and any q point with fewer than two usable values is skipped; the editor window reports how many curves and points were used, the fit model and output conventions, and, when a reference is used, its identity and the merge point.

Guinier quality check. After the curve is produced, the editor window reports a Guinier fit of it (a straight-line fit of ln I versus q2 over the low-q region, the window chosen so qmax·Rg ≤ 1.3): the radius of gyration Rg, I(0), and the fit's R2. This is a ground-truth-free quality signal — a high R2 with a physically sensible Rg indicates a clean low-q extrapolation; a low R2 is flagged and suggests checking the low-q data or the assigned concentrations.

I*(q) (already-normalized) curves

Curves whose name contains _Istarq_ are treated as already being in I*(q) form (header units g/mol, I(0) = MW), i.e. their intensity has already been normalized by concentration — even though their Conc: header carries the real, non-unity measurement concentration. For these curves the intensity is used as is (it is not divided by concentration again), while the real concentration is still used as the extrapolation variable. A selection made entirely of I*(q) curves therefore produces an already-normalized result (tagged Conc:1 in both modes). Mixing I*(q) curves with raw-intensity curves in one extrapolation is flagged with a warning, as the two are on different scales.
