SOMO - SAXS/SANS (SAS) Module: MALS

Last updated: February 2026

The Help for the MALS module, available from the July 2024 intermediate US-SOMO release, is currently under construction. We apologize for any inconvenience.

NOTICE: this module is being developed by E. Brookes and M. Rocco. The most recent (February 2026) developments will be subsequently published (Brookes et al., in preparation)

This new US-SOMO module is being developed for the analysis of Multi-Angle Light Scattering (MALS) data. In its current implementation, it is conceived for the advanced treatment of MALS data pre-produced by the ASTRA© software developed by Wyatt Technologies (now Waters; https://www.wyatt.com) for their HELEOS instruments. Therefore, users need to either use a version of the ASTRA© software to acquire and then pre-process their MALS data, or to convert their data in a format compatible with the US-SOMO MALS module. Differently from SAXS, which collects data over hundreds of scattering angles, commercial MALS detectors measure the light scattered at a relatively few angles (up to 18 for the Wyatt HELEOS-II© utilized for this module's development). The ASTRA© version utilized to collect the data was 6.1.7, that used to pre-process the data was version 7.2.3.19.

The first data needed in the MALS module is a list of the true scattering angles seen by each independent detector. In the HELEOS-II© instrument, the sample flows (or its loaded) in a thin channel bored through a solid cylindrical piece of glass, and the laser light is shined along this thin channel through sapphire windows at each end (the liquid is fed to the channel through two perpendicular HPLC-style connections). The detectors (high-gain solid state photodiodes) are positioned around the circular face of the cell cylindrical glass block. Due to the refraction at the liquid/glass interface, the actual scattering angles seen by each detector depend on both the refractive index of the glass and of the liquid (more informations can be found in the ASTRA© manual). Therefore, the real scattering angles are calculated from these refractive indices. Moreover, to compensate for the different photodiodes used and their different fields of view, a special procedure called normalization is usually performed everytime a different solvent is utilized in a MALS experiment. This is because detector #1 cannot be reached by scattered light because of the refractive index of the solvent used in this case, while detector #14 is replaced in the used HELEOS-II© instrument by a fiber optic for Quasi-Elastic Light Scattering measurements.
